Did Toronto get faster after the speed-camera ban?

In November 2025 the Province of Ontario banned Automated Speed Enforcement cameras. This dashboard uses the City of Toronto's network of ~1,200 driver-feedback radar signs to track whether speeding actually rose — by street, by ward, month over month — with a 2024 control to filter out seasonal noise.

A reader asked whether the analysis could be done spatially instead of just temporally. This grouping does exactly that, restricted to signs within 500 m of a former ASE camera point — the zone where there's a plausible mechanistic association between a specific camera and a driver's speed. Beyond ~500 m the "near vs far" framing breaks down (signs aren't being treated by any specific camera), so those bins are intentionally omitted. The DiD-adjusted bars also subtract the same windows in 2024 to strip seasonality. ASE locations published by the City include formerly-active and planned points; no install/removal dates are published, so "near" reads as "near a location designated for automated enforcement," not a guarantee enforcement was active.

A second question: did back-to-office mandates push downtown traffic up?

Volume DiD · Apr–Oct 2024→2025 vs same windows 2023→2024

Ontario's Public Service was ordered back to office at 3 days/week effective April 7, 2025 (federal Treasury Board mandate had landed earlier, in September 2024). To test whether the mandates measurably increased traffic, we compare the change in average daily volume at WYS signs in the 7 months after the OPS mandate (Apr–Oct 2025 vs Apr–Oct 2024) against the same year-over-year change in the prior year (Apr–Oct 2024 vs 2023) — a volume DiD.

WYS signs sit in safety / school zones, not on commuter arterials, so this measures spillover onto monitored side-streets in each area — not the Gardiner or DVP directly. Confounders include the federal RTO already in effect for the baseline year, private-sector mandates, and changes in WYS sign coverage year-over-year (which is why we aggregate at the area level, not per ward).

No causal claim is made about why measured speeds changed. The difference-in-differences method controls for prior-year seasonality but cannot rule out other simultaneous factors (weather variation, road construction, fleet composition, post-pandemic travel patterns, demographic shifts, etc.).

About the data's coverage: the radar logs measurements 24 hours a day, 7 days a week — including overnight and weekends. The "Weekdays from 7 AM – 9 PM" schedule listed on almost every sign refers to when the LED displays your speed back to drivers (the visible feedback period), not when the underlying speed counts are collected. This was verified by cross-checking the City's hourly-archive dataset against the monthly summary: the monthly volume per sign matches the sum of all 24-hour, 7-day-a-week hourly readings to within ~1%. That said, the WYS signs are placed in safety zones and on streets the City has flagged for feedback — they are not a uniform random sample of all Toronto streets, and city-level totals should be read as "speeding on streets with WYS signs," not "speeding on every road in Toronto."

How the numbers are computed

  • "% >10 km/h over the limit". For each monthly sign reading we sum vehicles in every 5 km/h bin whose lower boundary is ≥ posted_limit + 10 (e.g. for a 40 km/h zone, bins spd_50 and above), then divide by total volume. Consistent across all signs.
  • Year-over-year tier counts ("Total speeders" card). Same per-row bin rule, applied for each tier (10+, 15+, 20+, 25+, 30+ over). City totals include only the 733 signs that reported in both Jan-Apr 2025 and Jan-Apr 2026, so the comparison is apples-to-apples.
  • Aggregation: city and ward series are volume-weighted means across signs (a high-traffic arterial counts more than a quiet street). Per-sign series are direct monthly readings.
  • Difference-in-differences (DiD): the post-ban window (Dec 2025–Mar 2026) minus the pre-ban window (Jul–Oct 2025) — and the same delta in 2024 as a seasonal control — subtracted. The DiD number is the honest headline: it filters out year-over-year seasonality (winter weather, school sessions, etc.).
  • Spatial DiD by distance to ASE camera: signs are grouped into bins by great-circle distance (haversine) to their nearest published ASE camera point. The same pre/post 2025 windows are computed per bin (raw delta), and again with the 2024 control subtracted (DiD-adjusted). ASE locations as published include both formerly-active and planned points; the City does not publish install/removal dates, so "near an ASE camera" is best read as "near a location designated for automated enforcement at some point," not a guarantee enforcement was historically active.
  • Sign relocation: sign_id is intended to be stable, but a sign can occasionally be reassigned. Per-sign deltas on the most extreme outliers warrant a sanity-check against the address.
  • ASE camera dataset records current status only — Toronto does not publish install/removal dates. The Province's November 2025 ban removed automated enforcement city-wide, not just at the points shown on the map.
  • Hourly profiles are a 2023 baseline. Toronto stopped publishing the detailed hourly archive after 2023, so the "typical day" charts cannot be updated to the post-ban era — they show each sign's pre-ban traffic pattern of life.
